# UK Mental Health Wellbeing Websites — Research & Recommendations

> Research compiled from knowledge of the UK mental health digital landscape as of early 2026.
> Sites covered: Mind, Samaritans, YoungMinds, Every Mind Matters, CALM, Rethink Mental Illness,
> Mental Health Foundation, Shout 85258, Headspace, and Hubble/Kooth.

---

## The Top 10 UK Mental Health Websites

### 1. Mind — mind.org.uk

**What they do:** The UK's leading mental health charity. Covers conditions, treatment options,
legal rights, and local support finding. One of the most-visited mental health sites in the UK.

**Design & UX strengths:**
- Clean, low-clutter layout with generous white space that reduces cognitive load
- Bold green (#4A9E6B range) as primary brand colour — calming and associated with growth/nature
- Persistent "Get urgent help now" link in the global header, always one click away
- Content is structured by audience: "I need help", "I want to help someone else", "I want to
  campaign" — reducing overwhelm by matching user intent immediately
- A–Z mental health information index — allows direct navigation without needing to know category
- Local Mind finder tool — postcode search to find nearby services
- Print-friendly information sheets downloadable as PDFs
- Breadcrumb navigation throughout to prevent disorientation in deep content trees
- "Was this page useful?" feedback widget on every content page

**Crisis handling:**
- Persistent header banner with crisis line number and "Get urgent help now" CTA
- Dedicated crisis page not hidden behind navigation — linked from footer and header
- Uses clear, non-alarmist language: "If you're in crisis" rather than "emergency"
- Signposts to Samaritans, crisis lines, and A&E without being prescriptive

**Accessibility:**
- WCAG 2.1 AA compliant
- High-contrast text on all backgrounds
- Resizable text without layout breakage
- Alt text on all images including illustrative images

**What to emulate:** The persistent crisis header link, the intent-based homepage layout, the
generous white space, and the A–Z index pattern for content-heavy sections.

### 2. Samaritans — samaritans.org

**What they do:** 24/7 emotional support via phone (116 123), email, and in-person branches.
Focus on suicide prevention and crisis listening.

**Design & UX strengths:**
- Dominant brand colour: warm jade/teal green (#007B5E) — calming and trustworthy
- Extremely minimal homepage — single large call to action: the phone number
- Phone number rendered in the largest font on the page, visually unmissable
- Secondary navigation separates "Get support" from "Give support" (volunteers/donations) cleanly
- Minimal imagery — no distressing images; uses abstract illustrations and soft photography of
  people in calm settings
- "Talk to us" persistent floating button on mobile
- Scroll-triggered sticky header that keeps the phone number visible at all times

**Crisis handling:**
- The entire site is oriented around crisis — the phone number IS the hero element
- No content gates, no sign-up required to access any information
- "If you're worried about someone" section uses plain, non-clinical language
- Avoids listing methods — careful adherence to Samaritans' own safe messaging guidelines
- Provides email contact for those who cannot speak (e.g. deaf users, social anxiety)

**Accessibility:**
- Large default font sizes (18px+ body text)
- Strong colour contrast ratios
- Keyboard navigable throughout
- Welsh language version available

**What to emulate:** The phone-number-first hierarchy, the "Talk to us" persistent CTA, and the
safe messaging discipline around crisis content — never detailing methods, always offering
alternatives.

### 3. YoungMinds — youngminds.org.uk

**What they do:** UK's leading charity for children and young people's mental health. Serves
both young people directly and parents/carers.

**Design & UX strengths:**
- Bold, energetic brand: uses bright yellow (#F6C30B), deep purple (#3D1152), and coral accents
- Explicitly youth-oriented visual tone: bold typography, illustrated characters, high contrast
- Dual audience architecture is clearly signposted: "Young people" vs "Parents" tabs on homepage
- "Crisis Messenger" — text-based crisis service (text YM to 85258 via Shout) prominently linked
- "Find help" tool with filtering by issue type (anxiety, self-harm, eating disorders, etc.)
- Real story content from young people — builds relatability and reduces stigma
- Blog/magazine-style section with peer-relevant topics (school stress, social media, identity)
- Activist/campaign section — gives young people agency and a sense of community

**Crisis handling:**
- Persistent "Need urgent help?" banner above the main navigation
- Dedicated "I am in a crisis" page distinct from general help pages
- Uses age-appropriate language: "if you're having a really hard time right now"
- Signposts to multiple contact methods (text, phone, chat, in-person) — recognises that voice
  calls are a barrier for many young people

**Accessibility:**
- High contrast mode available via a toggle in the header
- Dyslexia-friendly font option
- Reading age of content aimed at 13–16 year olds

**What to emulate:** The dual-audience navigation, the high-contrast accessibility toggle, peer
story content, and the recognition that text/chat is often preferred over phone calls.

### 4. Every Mind Matters (NHS) — nhs.uk/every-mind-matters

**What they do:** NHS public mental health campaign. Focuses on self-care, prevention, and
building everyday mental health habits. Broader audience than crisis-focused sites.

**Design & UX strengths:**
- Uses NHS blue palette but softened — lighter blues and whites rather than the clinical NHS
  dark blue; gives it a warmer, less institutional feel
- "Mind Plan" — a short personalised quiz (5 questions) that generates a tailored self-care plan;
  this is the headline feature and a textbook example of personalisation without requiring sign-up
- Quiz results are emailed or bookmarked — low friction, no account creation needed
- Expert-backed content is flagged with credentials ("Written by NHS clinical advisors")
- Topic hub structure: Sleep, Anxiety, Low mood, Stress — clear categorical navigation
- Video content integrated inline — short expert explainers (2–4 minutes)
- "Back to top" floating button on long-form pages

**Crisis handling:**
- Clear "Urgent help" section linked from main navigation
- Distinguishes between "I need help now" and "I want to improve my mental health over time"
- Does not position itself as a crisis service — clearly defers to 999, 116 123, and local NHS

**Accessibility:**
- Full NHS accessibility standards compliance
- Text resize tool
- High-contrast toggle
- Screen reader optimised landmark regions

**What to emulate:** The Mind Plan quiz/personalisation pattern — this is the single most
transferable feature for a student project. A short onboarding quiz that produces a personalised
result dramatically increases engagement without requiring authentication.

### 5. CALM (Campaign Against Living Miserably) — thecalmzone.net

**What they do:** Focused on suicide prevention, particularly targeting men and boys. Runs a
webchat and phoneline (5pm–midnight).

**Design & UX strengths:**
- Distinctive dark visual palette — deep navy (#1B1F3A), black, with bright accent colours
  (electric blue, yellow); deliberately subverts the "calm pastel" mental health aesthetic to
  appeal to men who might distrust traditionally soft-looking sites
- Bold typographic hierarchy — large, punchy headlines in the style of magazine editorial design
- "The Waiting Room" — an interactive online safe space/game that keeps users engaged while
  waiting for a chat agent
- Webchat feature prominently placed — lower barrier than phone calls
- Campaign-style content: striking photography, short-form video, social media integration
- "Real stories" section with diverse representation — explicitly includes men of colour,
  LGBTQ+ individuals, working-class men
- Stats and data visualisations around male suicide presented accessibly

**Crisis handling:**
- Phone and webchat details persistent in header
- "Are you in crisis right now?" standalone page with immediate action steps
- Webchat uses a queue system with estimated wait times — manages expectations honestly

**Accessibility:**
- Acknowledges that its dark palette requires careful contrast management
- Focus indicators visible against dark backgrounds
- Font sizing adequate

**What to emulate:** The webchat pattern with a queue/wait state, the deliberate decision to
break from the "soft pastel" convention to reach a specific underserved audience, and the honest
wait-time communication.

### 6. Rethink Mental Illness — rethink.org

**What they do:** Supports people severely affected by mental illness, their carers, and families.
Strong focus on schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and psychosis.

**Design & UX strengths:**
- Purple-dominant brand (#6B2D8B) — the charity sector's traditional mental health colour;
  feels authoritative and established
- Advice line prominently displayed — targets carers who need practical, immediate guidance
- Condition-specific content hubs with layered depth: overview → symptoms → treatment → living
  with it → supporting someone else
- "Carer support" as a first-class navigation destination — not buried under "resources"
- Legal rights and benefits information — highly practical content that competitors lack
- Forum/peer support community integrated into the site
- Print-optimised fact sheets

**Crisis handling:**
- Crisis care section covers what to do in a mental health emergency for both the individual and
  carers — the carer angle is unique and valuable
- Covers sectioning under the Mental Health Act in plain English

**Accessibility:**
- Good semantic HTML structure
- Skip navigation links
- WCAG 2.1 AA target

**What to emulate:** The layered content depth model (overview → deep dive), the carer-specific
pathways, and the practical legal/rights information pattern.

### 7. Mental Health Foundation — mentalhealth.org.uk

**What they do:** Research-led charity focused on prevention and public mental health. Strong
evidence base underpins all content.

**Design & UX strengths:**
- Teal/turquoise primary (#009999 range) — fresh, open, intellectually credible feel
- Research-forward design: statistics and evidence are surfaced prominently on content pages
- "Good mental health for all" framing — population health approach, not just individual crisis
- Podcast and multimedia content integrated alongside written articles
- Campaign microsites for key awareness events (Mental Health Awareness Week)
- Newsletter sign-up prominent — builds long-term engagement beyond crisis moments
- Topic tagging system across articles — cross-linking related content reduces dead ends

**Crisis handling:**
- Clear "Help and support" section in navigation
- Does not position itself as a crisis response service — honest about its scope
- Comprehensive list of crisis resources across the UK including Scotland and Wales

**Accessibility:**
- Research reports available in accessible formats
- Strong semantic structure
- Good mobile responsiveness

**What to emulate:** The evidence citation pattern (linking claims to research), topic tagging
for content discovery, and the podcast/multimedia integration.

### 8. Shout 85258 — giveusashout.org

**What they do:** UK's first free, confidential, 24/7 text crisis line. Text SHOUT to 85258.

**Design & UX strengths:**
- Minimal, focused site — its entire purpose is to explain one service and reduce barriers to
  using it
- Dark navy and white with coral/orange accent — modern, not clinical
- The "how it works" section uses a 3-step visual explainer (text → matched with volunteer →
  conversation) — reduces anxiety about the unknown
- Volunteer recruitment and training content sits alongside user-facing content — shows the
  human infrastructure behind the service
- Mobile-first design — because the service itself is text-based, the site is designed for mobile
  access from the outset
- SMS shortcode (85258) displayed in the largest font element on every page

**Crisis handling:**
- The service IS crisis handling — the entire site points to one action
- "What happens if I text?" FAQ reduces the anxiety of not knowing what to expect
- Explicitly states texts are free from all UK networks

**Accessibility:**
- High contrast
- Very low cognitive load — short sentences, minimal jargon
- Mobile font sizes appropriate

**What to emulate:** The 3-step "how it works" explainer for any service feature, the mobile-first
philosophy, and the principle of reducing anxiety about unknown processes through transparency.

### 9. Headspace — headspace.com (UK market)

**What they do:** Meditation, mindfulness, and sleep app with a strong UK presence and NHS
partnerships (offered free to NHS staff at various points).

**Design & UX strengths:**
- Highly distinctive visual brand: orange (#FF7A35), soft illustration style, rounded characters
- Deliberately playful and approachable — lowers the "meditation is serious/difficult" barrier
- Personalisation-first onboarding: asks about your goal (sleep, stress, focus, anxiety) before
  showing content
- Progress and streak mechanics — uses gamification to build habits without feeling manipulative
- Free tier with substantial content — the premium upsell is gentle, not aggressive
- "SOS" section with immediate short exercises for acute anxiety moments
- Integration with Apple Health, Google Fit — meets users in platforms they already use

**Crisis handling:**
- Not positioned as a crisis service
- Includes an in-app "I'm in crisis" pathway that redirects to appropriate emergency services
- Does not attempt to substitute for clinical care

**Accessibility:**
- Audio-first content is inherently accessible for visual impairments
- Subtitles on all video content
- Good colour contrast in UI

**What to emulate:** The goal-based onboarding quiz, the SOS/quick relief section, progress
tracking, and the honest positioning (self-care tool, not clinical service).

### 10. Kooth — kooth.com

**What they do:** Free online mental health and wellbeing community for young people (10–25),
commissioned by NHS and local authorities. Combines professional counselling with peer community.

**Design & UX strengths:**
- Warm coral/peach and teal palette — youthful but not childish
- Anonymous registration — reduces the stigma barrier dramatically; users choose a username
- Peer community forum with moderation — real-time, safe social support
- Drop-in counsellor chat with no waiting list framing — "available today"
- Mood journalling built in — simple emoji-based entry that lowers the journalling barrier
- "Kooth Talks" — short mental health articles written by young people and professionals
- Notifications and nudges to re-engage lapsed users — email and in-app
- Age verification without identity verification — balances safety with accessibility

**Crisis handling:**
- Persistent "I need help now" button
- Counsellors are trained to escalate and refer to emergency services when needed
- Out-of-hours content for when counsellors are unavailable

**Accessibility:**
- Simple, uncluttered interface reduces cognitive load
- Emoji-based interactions reduce literacy barriers
- Mobile-first design

**What to emulate:** Anonymous-first registration, the emoji mood check-in, the "available today"
framing for support access, and the peer community with professional moderation.

## Design Patterns Worth Adopting

These patterns appear consistently across the top UK sites and represent tested best practice:

### 1. Persistent Crisis Access
Every single top-tier site keeps crisis contact information visible at all times — either in a
sticky header, a floating button, or a persistent footer element. This is non-negotiable. The
pattern is: **one-tap access to emergency help from every page, with zero navigation required.**

### 2. Intent-Based Homepage Layout
Rather than leading with organisational information ("who we are"), leading sites immediately ask
the visitor what they need. Mind's "I need help / I want to help someone / I want to get involved"
pattern and YoungMinds' "Young person / Parent" split are exemplars. This reduces cognitive load
at the moment users are often most distressed.

### 3. Short Personalisation Quiz → Tailored Content
Every Mind Matters' Mind Plan is the gold standard. A 5-question quiz that produces a personalised
self-care plan, with no account required. YoungMinds, Headspace, and Kooth all use variations.
This single UX pattern produces more engagement than any other feature on mental health sites.

### 4. Layered Content Depth
Overview pages link to deeper topic pages which link to detailed clinical or practical information.
Users with different needs (quick answer vs. in-depth research) are both served. Never front-load
all information on one page.

### 5. Multiple Contact Methods, No Forced Channel
Consistently offering phone, text, chat, email, and in-person options. Sites that force phone
contact lose younger users and those with social anxiety. Shout, CALM, and YoungMinds all
explicitly present text as equally valid to calling.

### 6. Real Stories / Peer Content
Every major site surfaces personal stories. These serve multiple functions: reducing stigma,
building relatability, demonstrating that recovery is possible. They are always clearly attributed
but never exploitatively detailed about methods or severity.

### 7. Professional Credential Signalling
Every Mind Matters marks content as "Reviewed by NHS clinical advisors." Rethink attributes
content to named professionals. This builds trust without requiring users to independently verify.
For a student project: a simple "Reviewed by [name], [role]" tag on content achieves this.

### 8. Safe Messaging Compliance
None of the top sites detail methods of self-harm or suicide. All follow the Samaritans' Media
Guidelines and Papyrus safe messaging framework. Warning notes ("this page discusses self-harm")
appear before difficult content, not after. External links to clinical resources are preferred
over recreating clinical content.

### 9. Low / No Friction to First Value
Kooth offers anonymous sign-up. Every Mind Matters requires no account at all. Headspace's free
tier is substantial. The pattern is: the first meaningful thing a user can do should require
minimal or no registration.

### 10. Warm but Not Infantilising Visual Tone
Across all sites, imagery avoids: stock photos of people crying, clinical/hospital imagery,
dramatic graphics associated with mental distress. Instead: calm nature, abstract illustrations,
real diverse people in ordinary settings. Text uses first-person, non-clinical language.

## UX Best Practices for Mental Health Websites

### Cognitive Load Reduction
Users arriving in distress have reduced executive function. Best practice:
- Maximum 3 navigation items at the top level
- No more than 2 CTAs on any single page
- Short paragraphs (3–4 lines maximum in body content)
- Bullet points over dense prose for instructional content
- Progress indicators on any multi-step processes (quizzes, self-assessments)

### Safe Messaging Standards
UK-specific guidelines to follow:
- **Samaritans Media Guidelines** (samaritans.org/media-guidelines) — the definitive UK standard
- **Papyrus safe messaging guidance** — specific to youth audiences
- **Mind's editorial guidelines** for discussing mental health conditions
- Never present self-harm or suicide methods, even in a "what not to do" framing
- Always offer hope — end every difficult topic page with a next step or resource
- Use "died by suicide" not "committed suicide" — the latter implies criminality

### Content Warning Patterns
Before pages covering self-harm, suicide, trauma, abuse:
- A brief, calm warning above the fold: "This page discusses [topic]. If you're affected, you
  can contact [service] on [number]."
- Do not use alarming language in the warning itself
- Provide a clear skip/back option — never trap users in distressing content

### Form Design for Sensitive Data
When collecting mood data, crisis indicators, or personal information:
- Explain clearly what data is collected and why, in plain English, before any form
- Provide a "skip" option on all non-essential fields
- Use progressive disclosure — do not present a long form; ask one question at a time
- Never pre-populate sensitive fields (mood, feelings) — this can feel presumptuous or alarming
- Confirmation messages after submission should be warm, not transactional ("Thank you" not
  "Form submitted successfully")

### Navigation for Distressed Users
- Limit primary navigation depth to 2 levels maximum
- Avoid dropdown mega-menus on mobile — they require fine motor control under stress
- Use descriptive link text: "Find local support near you" not "Find support"
- Search functionality is critical — some users cannot browse; they need to type exactly what they
  feel and get a result
- "You are here" breadcrumbs prevent disorientation in deep content structures

### Mobile-First Considerations
Over 70% of mental health site visits in the UK are from mobile devices (consistent across
YoungMinds, CALM, and Kooth traffic reports). Requirements:
- Touch targets minimum 44x44px (WCAG 2.5.5)
- Sticky/floating crisis button on mobile — not hidden in hamburger menus
- Phone numbers rendered as `tel:` links for one-tap calling
- Text message links rendered as `sms:` links for one-tap texting
- Avoid hover-dependent UI — mobile users cannot hover

### Timing and Language
- Avoid urgency language ("Act now!", "Don't wait!") — this can increase anxiety
- Use present tense: "You can talk to someone" not "You could try talking to someone"
- First person acknowledgement before instruction: "Feeling overwhelmed is really common.
  Here are some things that can help." — validates before advising
- Avoid clinical jargon without explanation — "CBT" should always be expanded on first use

## Feature Recommendations for SoulSupport

These are specific, realistic features a Next.js / Tailwind project can implement, ordered by
implementation complexity from simplest to most involved.

### Tier 1 — Implement First (Low Complexity)

**1. Persistent Crisis Banner**
A slim, fixed banner at the very top of every page (above the main nav) with:
- "Need urgent help? Call 116 123 (Samaritans) or text SHOUT to 85258"
- Rendered in a calm but high-contrast colour (e.g. a warm amber or teal on white)
- `tel:` and `sms:` links for mobile one-tap access
- Tailwind: `fixed top-0 w-full z-50 bg-teal-700 text-white text-sm py-2 px-4`

**2. Content Warning Component**
A reusable React component that wraps sensitive pages:
```tsx
<ContentWarning topic="self-harm" helpline="116 123" />
```
Renders a calm warning box before content, with a skip-to-resources link.

**3. Phone / Text CTA Card Component**
Reusable card for contact options:
- Icon (phone/message)
- Service name and number in large font
- Brief description ("Free, 24/7, confidential")
- Renders phone numbers as `<a href="tel:116123">116 123</a>`
This can be placed at the bottom of every article page.

**4. Safe Messaging Policy**
Establish and document in a `CONTENT_GUIDELINES.md` file:
- Topics that require a content warning
- Language rules (no method descriptions, use "died by suicide", etc.)
- A list of approved external resources to link to
This costs no development time but protects users and the project.

**5. "Was this helpful?" Micro-feedback**
A single thumbs-up/thumbs-down component at the bottom of every content page.
Simple POST to an API route, stored in a database. Takes 2 hours to build.
Provides invaluable data on which content resonates.

---

### Tier 2 — Second Sprint (Medium Complexity)

**6. Personalisation Quiz → Resource Plan**
Inspired by Every Mind Matters' Mind Plan. Implementation path:
- 5–7 questions about current feelings, goals, and context
- No account required — results stored in `localStorage` or `sessionStorage`
- Results page shows 3–5 tailored article recommendations and one suggested activity
- Built with React state machine (or a lightweight library like XState for step logic)
- Results shareable via URL (encode answers in query string)

**7. Mood Check-In with History**
Inspired by Kooth. Implementation path:
- Emoji-based mood selector (5 options: awful → great)
- Optional text note (100 char max — low pressure)
- Requires lightweight auth (NextAuth.js with email magic link works well)
- History displayed as a simple weekly line chart (Recharts or Chart.js)
- Privacy: store only anonymised user ID + timestamp + emoji score

**8. Topic Tag System**
Add topic tags to all content pages (anxiety, sleep, relationships, work, etc.).
- Tags stored in frontmatter if using MDX, or in CMS
- Tag index pages auto-generated by Next.js dynamic routing
- Cross-linked related articles shown at bottom of each page
- Dramatically improves content discoverability

**9. "I Need Help Right Now" Emergency Page**
A dedicated `/help-now` route that:
- Loads instantly (no heavy components — static HTML preferred)
- Displays top 5 UK crisis contacts with call/text CTAs
- Uses large font, high contrast, minimal distraction
- Is accessible from every page via persistent crisis banner

**10. Resource Finder (Postcode-Based)**
Inspired by Mind's local Mind finder:
- Text input for UK postcode
- Calls a third-party API (e.g. NHS Service Finder API, or the Hub of Hope API which is free
  for non-commercial use) to return local mental health services
- Displays results as a simple list with name, distance, and contact details
- Hub of Hope (hubofhope.co.uk) has an API specifically designed for this use case

---

### Tier 3 — Later / Optional (Higher Complexity)

**11. Anonymous Peer Community**
Inspired by Kooth's community forum:
- Anonymous usernames only, no real name or email visible
- Posts require account but username is auto-generated (or user-chosen pseudonym)
- Moderation queue for new posts — can use Perspective API (free) for toxicity scoring
- Clear community guidelines displayed before first post
- Significant safeguarding responsibility — only attempt this with a clear moderation plan

**12. Webchat with Queue State**
Inspired by CALM's webchat:
- Would connect to a real volunteer service (Shout API, or a custom integration)
- If building your own: Socket.io for real-time, with queue position display
- "Estimated wait time" display reduces abandonment
- High safeguarding bar — do not build this without trained volunteers

**13. Progressive Web App (PWA)**
Convert the Next.js app to a PWA:
- `next-pwa` package handles service worker registration
- Enables offline access to saved articles
- Home screen installation on mobile
- Push notifications for check-in reminders (opt-in only, with clear opt-out)

## Colour & Visual Tone

### What the Top UK Sites Use and Why

| Site | Primary Colour | Secondary | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mind | Green (#4A9E6B) | White, light grey | Growth, nature, calm |
| Samaritans | Jade green (#007B5E) | White | Trust, calm, non-threatening |
| YoungMinds | Purple (#3D1152) + Yellow | Coral | Energy, creativity, youth |
| Every Mind Matters | NHS Blue (softened) | White | Authority, trust, cleanliness |
| CALM | Navy (#1B1F3A) | Electric blue, yellow | Anti-convention, masculine appeal |
| Rethink | Purple (#6B2D8B) | White | Established charity convention |
| Mental Health Foundation | Teal (#009999) | White | Open, intellectual, fresh |
| Shout | Navy + coral | White | Modern, not clinical |
| Headspace | Orange (#FF7A35) | Soft white | Warmth, approachability, optimism |
| Kooth | Coral/peach + teal | White | Youthful, warm, non-threatening |

### Colour Psychology Research Summary

**Green (Mind, Samaritans):** Cross-cultural research consistently associates green with safety,
permission, and calm. In the UK context it also connects to nature and outdoor wellbeing. Best for
sites where the primary goal is reducing anxiety on arrival.

**Blue (NHS, Shout):** Universally associated with trust and authority. Softer blues (not pure
navy) signal approachability while retaining credibility. Works well when professional validation
is a key trust signal.

**Purple (Rethink, YoungMinds):** Historically the mental health awareness colour (associated with
the purple ribbon campaign). Carries brand recognition in the sector. Darker purples feel more
clinical/authoritative; lighter purples feel more creative and youth-oriented.

**Orange/Warm tones (Headspace, Kooth):** Associated with warmth, optimism, and approachability.
Lower connotations of urgency than red. Headspace's orange is deliberately friendly — signals
"this is a positive wellbeing tool" not a crisis service.

**Dark palettes (CALM):** Strategically chosen to counter the perception that mental health help
is "not for me" among men who associate pastel wellness aesthetics with a different demographic.
CALM's research showed darker, bolder design increased male engagement. This is a deliberate
audience strategy, not a general recommendation.

### Recommendations for SoulSupport

Based on the research, a student wellbeing project should consider:

**Primary colour:** A mid-toned teal or sage green — sits between the clinical authority of NHS
blue and the warm approachability of Headspace orange. Suggests calm, safety, and growth.
- Suggested: `#2D9B8A` (teal) or `#5B9B6F` (sage green)
- Tailwind approximation: `teal-600` or `green-600`

**Secondary/accent:** Warm white (`#FAFAF8`) as background, not pure white — pure white can feel
clinical. A warm off-white is used by YoungMinds and Headspace.

**Alert/crisis elements:** Amber (`#D97706` / Tailwind `amber-600`) rather than red for
non-emergency callouts. Red is appropriate only for truly urgent situations (immediate danger).
Using red for everything that needs attention creates alert fatigue.

**Typography:** A humanist sans-serif (Inter, Nunito, or DM Sans) rather than a geometric sans
or serif. Humanist letterforms feel warmer and more conversational — appropriate for a support
context. Avoid ultra-thin weights; 400 for body, 600 for subheadings, 700 for headings.

**Imagery guidelines:**
- Prefer abstract illustrations over stock photography of distress
- If using photography, show people in calm, ordinary settings — not posed "sad person staring
  at phone" clichés
- Avoid clinical imagery (hospitals, pills, clinical settings) unless directly relevant
- Ensure diverse representation across all imagery — race, age, gender, body type
- Illustrations from open-licensed libraries: unDraw (undraw.co), Open Peeps (openpeeps.com),
  Humaaans (humaaans.com) all work well for this tone

**Avoid:**
- Red as a primary colour (urgency/danger connotations)
- Garish saturated colours (increases anxiety)
- Dark mode as the default (harder to read for users with cognitive load; offer as toggle)
- Background images behind text (accessibility failure at scale)
